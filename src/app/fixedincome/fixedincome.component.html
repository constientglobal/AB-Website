Fixed income securities are debt instruments that pay investors a fixed stream of income over a specified period, typically returning the principal amount at maturity. They are known for their potential to provide stability and regular returns, making them attractive to investors seeking income and capital preservation.

What is Fixed Income?

Fixed income refers to investments that provide a predictable stream of payments to the investor. When you invest in a fixed income security, you are essentially lending money to a borrower (such as a government, corporation, or municipality) in exchange for regular interest payments and the repayment of your principal at a future date.

Common types of fixed income securities include:

  • Bonds: The most common form, issued by governments (government bonds, treasury bills) or corporations (corporate bonds).
  • Debentures: Similar to bonds, often unsecured.
  • Certificates of Deposit (CDs): Time deposits offered by banks.
  • Money Market Instruments: Short-term debt securities like commercial papers and treasury bills.
  • Fixed Deposits (FDs): Offered by banks, providing a fixed interest rate for a set period.
Why Invest in Fixed Income?

Fixed income investments offer several compelling advantages for investors, particularly those focused on stability and income:

  1. Regular Income Stream: They provide predictable and consistent interest payments, which can be a valuable source of income for investors, especially retirees.
  2. Capital Preservation: Fixed income securities are generally considered less volatile than equities, aiming to preserve the original investment amount, especially if held to maturity.
  3. Diversification: Adding fixed income to a portfolio can help reduce overall risk, as bonds often perform differently than stocks, especially during market downturns.
  4. Lower Volatility: Compared to equities, fixed income investments typically exhibit lower price fluctuations, providing a more stable portfolio component.
  5. Portfolio Stability: They can act as a ballast in a diversified portfolio, cushioning against sharp declines in equity markets.
  6. Predictability: The fixed nature of payments and maturity dates offers a clear outlook on future returns.
Key Concepts in Fixed Income Investing
  • Principal (Face Value/Par Value): The amount of money the borrower promises to repay the investor at maturity.
  • Coupon Rate (Interest Rate): The fixed annual interest rate paid on the bond's face value.
  • Maturity Date: The date on which the principal amount of the bond is repaid to the investor.
  • Yield to Maturity (YTM): The total return an investor can expect to receive if they hold the bond until it matures.
  • Credit Rating: An assessment of the borrower's ability to repay its debt.
  • Duration: A measure of a bond's price sensitivity to changes in interest rates.
  • Bond Price: The market price at which a bond trades.
  • Government Bonds (G-Secs): Issued by central or state governments, generally considered low-risk.
  • Corporate Bonds: Issued by companies to raise capital, carrying varying levels of credit risk.
Risks Associated with Fixed Income Investing
  • Interest Rate Risk: If market interest rates rise, the value of existing bonds tends to fall.
  • Credit Risk (Default Risk): The risk that the borrower may not repay the principal or interest.
  • Inflation Risk: The risk that inflation will erode purchasing power of fixed payments.
  • Liquidity Risk: Some bonds may be difficult to sell quickly without price impact.
  • Reinvestment Risk: The risk that future interest payments may be reinvested at lower rates.
